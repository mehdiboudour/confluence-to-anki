  • What creatures generate this natural cycle called the circadian rhythm?
    • Most living creatures on the planet with a life span of more than several days.
  • Where is the internal twenty-four-hour clock located?
    • Deep within the brain.
  • What parts of the body receive the daily circadian rhythm signal beamed from the internal twenty-four-hour clock?
    • Every other region of the brain and every organ in the body.
  • The twenty-four-hour tempo controls other rhythmic patterns, besides sleep and wakefulness, such as,
    • Preferences for eating and drinking.
    • Moods and emotions.
    • The amount of produces urine.
    • Core body temperature.
    • Metabolic rate.
    • The release of numerous hormones.
  • What are important life events observed to have circadian rhythmicity.
    • Breaking an Olympic record.

    • It has been clearly tied to early afternoons where human circadian rhythm being maximal at its natural peak.
  • How can births and deaths be tied to the circadian rhythm?
    • Due to marked swings in life-dependent metabolic, cardiovascular, temperature, and hormonal processes that it controls.
  • Who discovered the very first evidence that plants generate their own internal time.
    • French geophysicist Jean-Jacques d’Ortous de Mairan.
  • When did de Mairan discover the very first evidence that plants generate their own internal time?
    • In 1729.
  • What is heliotropism?
    • When a plant’s leaves or flowers track the trajectory of the sun as it moves across the sky during the day.
  • What plant did de Mairan study in order to make the discovery of self generated internal time?
    • Mimosa pudica.
  • What was the particularity of Mimosa Pudica, besides manifesting heliotropism?
    • Its leaves collapse down during the night as if they wilted.
  • What does the word pudica mean?
    • From the Latin meaning “shy” or “bashful”.
  • Why was Mimosa pudica named as such?
    • Because the leaves would collapse down if you touch or stroke them.
  • What did the famous evolutionary biologist Charles Darwin call Mimosa pudica?
    • Sleeping leaves.
  • Before De Marian’s discovery, what was the belief of Mimosa pudica's opening and folding behaviour of its leaves?
    • That it was solely triggered by sunlight, open during the day, and folded during the night.
  • How did de Mairan perform his experiment on Mimosa pudica to prove it has an endogenous rhythm?
    • He placed the plant in a sealed box for 24 hours.
  • What did De Marian observe from placing Mimosa pudica in a sealed box for 24 hours?
    • That the plant displayed the same behaviour of opening and folding with the (almost) same timing, despite being cut off from the sun’s signal.
  • What is an endogenous rhythm?
    • A self-generated rhythm.
  • Why is de Mairan’s discovery of the endogenous rhythm of Mimosa pudica so revolutionary?
    • He had shown, for the first time, that a living organism kept its own time, and was not slave to the sun’s rhythmic commands.
  • What is another endogenous rhythm of the body, other than the circadian rhythm?
    • The heat beat.
  • What is difference in scale between the heat beat and the circadian rhythms?
    • The heart’s pacemaker rhythm is faster, usually beating at least once a second, rather than once every twenty-four-hour period like the circadian clock.
  • Since de Mairan’s discovery, how long did it take to prove that humans have a similar internally generated circadian rhythm as Mimosa pudica?
    • Another two hundred years.
  • Who first discovered that humans have an internally generated circadian rhythm?
    • Professor Nathaniel Kleitman at the University of Chicago and his research assistant Bruce Richardson.
  • What year did Kleitman and Richardson first discover that humans generated a circadian rhythm?
    • In 1938.
  • What did the experiment of Kleitman and Richardson in 1938 consist of?
    • Isolate themselves from sunlight for an extensive amount of time in a cave and observe their wake/sleep cycle.
  • In what cave did Kleitman and Richardson go into in 1938 to perform their experiment?
    • Mammoth Cave in Kentucky, one of the deepest caverns on the planet.
  • Why did Kleitman and Richardson choose Mammoth Cave to perform their experiment on sleep in 1938?
    • Because it is so deep that no detectable sunlight penetrates its farthest reaches.
  • How long did Kleitman and Richardson last in Mammoth Cave in 1938 for their experiment?
    • Thirty-two days.
  • Kleitman and Richardson made two groundbreaking discoveries inside Mammoth Cave in 1938?
    • Humans generate their own endogenous circadian rhythm in the absence of external light from the sun.
    • Human biological rhythm is approximately twenty-four-hours in length, and not precisely that.
  • Inside Mammoth Cave, in 1938, Kleitman and Richardson expressed a predictable and repeating pattern of,
    • Prolonged wakefulness, about fifteen hours.
    • Consolidated bouts of about nine hours of sleep.
  • Kleitman and Richardson developed the following sleep-wake cycles in Mammoth Cave in 1938.
    • Richardson, in his twenties, between 26 and 28 hours.
    • Kleitman in his fortie, was a little closer, but still longer than, 24 hours.
  • How long did science determine a human adult’s endogenous circadian clock to run, in the seventy-plus years since Kleitman and Richardson’s seminal experiment?
    • Around 24 hours and 15 minutes in average.
  • If the human circadian rhythm is approximately and not precisely 24 hours in length, how come we don’t experience a delay in our lives?
    • The light of the sun methodically resets our inaccurate internal timepiece, “winding“ us back to precisely, not approximately, 24 hours.
  • Why does the brain use daylight for the resetting purpose of the circadian rhythm?
    • It is the most reliable, repeating signal that we have in our environment.
  • How reliable is daylight as a repeating signal in our environment?
    • Since the birth of our planet and every single day thereafter, the sun has has risen in the morning and set in the evening without fail.
  • Why did most living species likely adopt a circadian rhythm?
    • To synchronize themselves and their activities, both internal (e.g., temperature) and external (e.g., feeding) with the daily orbital mechanics of planet Earth.
  • The can also use other external cues, other than sunlight, such as,
    • Food.
    • Exercise.
    • Temperature fluctuations.
    • Regularly timed social interaction.
  • Why do individuals with certain forms of blindness not entirely lose their circadian rhythm?
    • Because their brain uses other external cues, other than sunlight to reset its internal clock.
  • The phenomenon of an imprecise biological clock in other species than humans has brought the following observations,
    • Internal biological clocks have been consistently observed in many different species to be imprecise.
    • It is not consistently longer than 24 hours in all species, for some it is shorter when places in total darkness, such as hamsters and squirrels.
  • if sunlight resets our circadian rhythm, how does it reset on an overcast day?
    • Even sunlight coming through thick clouds on a rainy day is powerful enough to help reset it.
  • What is a zeitgeber?
    • Any signal that the brain uses for the purpose of clock resetting.
  • Where does the term zeitgeber come from?
    • From the German “time giver”or “synchronizer”.
  • What is the twenty-four-hour biological clock sitting in the middle of the brain called?
    • The suprachiasmatic nucleus.
  • What is the meaning of the anatomical term “suprachiasmatic“?
    • supra, meaning above, and chiasm, meaning a crossing point.
  • What is the is the suprachiasmatic nucleus named as such? (supra=above, chiasm=crossing point)
    • Because it is located in the middle of the brain just above the intersection of the optic nerves coming from the eyeballs.
  • Why is the suprachiasmatic nucleus located just above the crossing point of the optic nerves?
    • In order to sample the light signal sent by each eye along the optic nerves before they head back to the back of the brain for visual processing.
  • Why does the suprachiasmatic nucleus sample the sunlight signal along the optic nerves?
    • In order to reset its inherent time inaccuracy to 24 hours cycle using this reliable light informations.

    • 20 000.

    • Approximately 100 billion neurons.
  • For diurnal species, such as humans, the circadian rhythm activates many brain and body mechanism that are designed to,
    • Keep you awake and alert during daylight hours.
    • Removing that alerting influence at nighttime by ratcheting down those mechanisms.
  • The changes in core body temperatures driven by the circadian rhythm follow the following pattern for the average human adult.
    • It begins rise around noon.
    • It peaks late in the afternoon.
    • It begins to decline again after that.
    • It drops below the midday start-point as bedtime approaches.
    • It reaches its nadir, or low point, about 2 hours after sleep onset.
  • How does core body temperature of a human fluctuate in case there is a sleep schedule alteration?
    • The temperature rhythm is no dependent upon whether one is asleep or not, it will rise and fall regardless.
  • Is body temperature the only rhythm dictated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus?
    • No, it is just one of many 24 hour rhythms that it governs.
  • What influences the other between sleep/wakefulness and the circadian rhythm?
    • Wakefulness and sleep are under the control of the circadian rhythm and not the other way around.
